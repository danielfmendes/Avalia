Avalia turns twelve years of Portuguese property listings, plus tourism, search, and macro signals, into parish-level intelligence and twelve-month forecasts for Lisbon.

% ---------- 1. Introduction ----------
\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Idealista, Imovirtual, and Casa Sapo only show \emph{current} listings; none surfaces parish-level (\emph{freguesia}) price history or the macro-economic and behavioural signals that move the market.

\textbf{User stories.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item As a \emph{renter or buyer}, I want decade-long price history for my parish, to negotiate from evidence.
  \item As a \emph{small landlord or agent}, I want year-on-year parish benchmarks, to price listings competitively.
  \item As a \emph{municipal planner}, I want housing-pressure indicators correlated with tourism and search, to target interventions.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Why this is a Big Data project.} The base dataset is 2.6\,GB of raw listings ($\sim$3\,M transactions) from \emph{arquivo.pt} via the open-data project \emph{habitacao.net}, joined with Google Trends and PORDATA at weekly-to-annual cadences (Section~\ref{sec:data}). Volume, Variety, and Veracity are present from day one; Velocity and full Value are analysed in Section~\ref{sec:fivevs}.

% ---------- 2. System ----------
\section{System architecture and reproducibility}\label{sec:system}

Seven pages share a D3-Geo Lisbon map that drills district~$\to$~municipality~$\to$~parish, filterable by sale type and room count:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Market Overview}: district prices, KPIs, drill-down map.
  \item \emph{AI Predictions}: 12-month per-region forecast with uncertainty bands.
  \item \emph{Signals}: price overlaid on Google Trends, \emph{dormidas}, earnings, construction.
  \item \emph{Compare}: side-by-side, up to six regions.
  \item \emph{Rooms}: T0--T4+ trends per typology.
  \item \emph{Affordability}: budget slider ranking parishes in reach.
  \item \emph{Time Machine}: monthly scrubber back to 2012.
\end{itemize}

% ---------- 3. Data sources ----------
\section{Data sources}\label{sec:data}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{arquivo.pt + habitacao.net.} \emph{arquivo.pt} (Portugal's web archive) snapshots Idealista, Imovirtual, and Casa Sapo periodically; \emph{habitacao.net} mines those snapshots to reconstruct listings history back to 2012 with price, area, rooms, and location per entry.
  \item \textbf{Google Trends.} Weekly Lisbon housing-keyword search interest, a leading indicator: spikes in queries like ``comprar casa'' precede price moves by one to two months.
  \item \textbf{PORDATA.} Per-municipality series: tourism overnights (\emph{dormidas}, monthly) as a short-term-rental-pressure proxy, plus four annual macro series (foreign workers, tourist capacity, mean earnings, completed constructions).
\end{itemize}

% ---------- 4. Forecasting ----------
\section{Forecasting model}\label{sec:forecast}\label{sec:differentiation}

Per region, the frontend fits OLS to the trailing 18 monthly €/m\textsuperscript{2} averages and projects 12 months ahead client-side, with the uncertainty band widening 1.5\,\% per step.

\textbf{What is different.} Where Idealista shows the present, Avalia shows \emph{history} (\emph{Time Machine} scrubs back to 2012) and \emph{future} (12-month projections), and overlays exogenous signals on price curves so users \emph{see} what moves the market.

\textbf{AI in the build.} Code generation was AI-assisted (Claude Code); a representative prompt log is in Appendix~\ref{app:prompts}.

\textbf{Planned upgrade.} The current model extrapolates each region's own past prices and misses turning points; the next version adds Google Trends, \emph{dormidas}, and the PORDATA macro series as exogenous predictors so the forecast reflects live demand and macro context.

\textbf{Growth estimate.} Portuguese real-estate transactions run at $\sim$150--200\,k purchases per year. The forward plan is a \textbf{weekly \emph{Idealista} snapshot}: every seven days the scraper pulls active listings per enabled region and the ETL re-aggregates them into the existing schema. Sizing from Lisbon's observed density (4.1 rows per parish per month, 88:1 raw\,$\to$\,aggregated compression) and applying it across the other regions' parish counts (rural parishes are far sparser), the corpus grows from 65\,k rows today to roughly \textbf{0.45\,M after three years} and $\sim$0.6\,M by 2030 -- an order-of-magnitude expansion at constant Cloudflare D1 cost. Every gap in Table~\ref{tab:fivevs} is bridged by a known technology. Figure~\ref{fig:growth} traces the trajectory; dashed lines mark regional rollouts.

% ---------- 6. Discussion ----------
\section{Discussion and conclusion}\label{sec:roadmap}

\subsection{Monetisation and roadmap}

Three revenue lines are plausible: a freemium consumer tier with a paid \emph{investor} mode, a B2B API for banks and agencies, and anonymised market reports for municipalities. Next business steps: a paid validation pilot with one B2B partner (a small agency or appraisal firm) to test API willingness-to-pay, tiered usage-based pricing, and a GDPR / data-licensing review before any municipal contract.

\subsection{Limitations}\label{sec:limits}

Cloudflare D1's free tier caps writes at \textbf{100\,000 rows per day} \parencite{cloudflareD1}, which is why the prototype is scoped to the eighteen Lisbon AML municipalities; aggregation compresses $\sim$3\,M raw rows to $\sim$65\,k, inside the cap. The forecast does not yet use the exogenous regressors, and refresh is manual.

\subsection{Problem encountered: Lisboa-level aggregation}

Early in the build the district-level view showed zero rows for Lisbon city. Inspecting the data, Lisboa turned out to be the only municipality whose pre-aggregated ``Grouped at Municipio level'' marker rows are absent from the source dataset; every other municipality has them, but for Lisboa only the underlying parish rows exist. A naive \texttt{WHERE freguesia = 'Grouped at Municipio level'} therefore filters Lisboa out entirely, leaving a blank tile on the district map. The fix synthesises Lisboa's municipality row on the fly at query time, weighted by \texttt{total\_rows}. Cloudflare D1, which is SQLite-based and runs at the edge, does not handle correlated subqueries reliably for this shape, so the implementation uses \texttt{UNION ALL}. The resulting branch lives in \texttt{api/src/index.ts} under the \texttt{level=district} path.

\prompt{In our D1-backed Hono Worker, GET /api/search?level=district returns no rows for Lisbon city. Every other municipality has 'Grouped at Municipio level' rows in the habitacao table, but Lisboa has only parish rows. Rewrite the SQL so the district response includes Lisboa, computed on the fly as a weighted aggregate of its parish rows: averages are SUM(metric * total\_rows) / SUM(total\_rows), and total\_rows itself is summed. D1's correlated-subquery support is unreliable, so combine the existing district rows with the synthesised Lisboa row using UNION ALL. Group the synthesised side by mes\_ano, tipo\_venda, tipo\_habitacao, quartos, distrito, municipio. Order the final result by mes\_ano ASC.}

\subsection{Secondary problem: parish-name mismatch on the Lisbon map}

A related, smaller area-level issue: the choropleth had blank parishes for many of Lisboa's \emph{freguesias}. The raw GeoJSON used parish names in slightly different casing, diacritics, and hyphen-versus-space conventions than the \texttt{freguesia} strings coming back from \texttt{/api/search} (for example \emph{Avenidas Novas} versus \emph{Avenidas-Novas}). The fix was to simplify the GeoJSON and route every name through a single normaliser before comparison.

\prompt{In LisbonMap.tsx the choropleth fill is empty for several parishes because the names in lisbon-parishes.json don't exactly match the freguesia strings returned by /api/search. Both sources use Portuguese with diacritics and inconsistent hyphen-vs-space conventions. Write a normalizeName helper that lowercases the input, strips diacritics, and replaces every run of non-alphanumeric characters with a single hyphen; then key the fill lookup by the normalised name on both sides so the map and the API agree.}
